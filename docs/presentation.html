The research bottleneck: why automate, and what must not be lost

Pain points in converter research

  • Expert time is the scarce resource — topology derivation, controller design and simulation debugging are iteration-heavy, senior-level work.
  • Design spaces explode combinatorially: switch states, topologies, modulation choices — exhaustive human coverage is impractical.
  • Verification burden: every claim needs waveforms, counts, and standards-compliant metrics; errors hide in normalization conventions and boundary cases.
  • Reproducibility: results scattered across reports, scripts and memories of departed students.

Requirements for an automated substitute

  • Run experiments on real simulation physics, not statistical guesswork.
  • Produce conclusions with evidence trails a human can walk.
  • Catch its own errors — and convert each one into a permanent check.
  • Preserve and reuse the laboratory's accumulated knowledge.

The rest of this deck is the system that meets these four requirements.

Terminology in three minutes

Power electronics

The engineering of electric-power conversion: semiconductor switches operate at tens of kilohertz; inductors and capacitors smooth the result.

THD (total harmonic distortion) quantifies residual waveform impurity; grid practice targets < 5%.

Two canonical converter families: VSC (voltage-source) and CSC (current-source) — structural mirror twins.

Graph theory

A circuit maps to a graph: components → edges, junctions → nodes.

Graphs with identical wiring under renaming are isomorphic; the lab's graph-based translation converts designs between VSC and CSC mechanically — controllers included.

AI agents & MCP

An agent is a language model granted execution: files, programs, retrieval. Complex tasks fan out to ~20 agents in parallel.

MCP is the standard interface by which agents operate professional software — here, a locally installed MATLAB/Simulink R2026a. All waveforms shown are genuine simulation output.

Language models can assert falsehoods fluently ("hallucination") — the central design threat.

Four house terms used later

Arc — one complete research unit: question → experiment → two independent audits → archived conclusion. Think "one mini-paper with its review attached".

Referee / Challenger — two AI auditors per arc: the referee recomputes every number from scratch; the challenger tries to break the interpretation.

Canon — textbook facts that were re-derived by machine before we allow anything to be graded against them.

Graph translation — the mechanical VSC ↔ CSC conversion recipe (a one-line idea: convert a voltage-source design into its current-source twin by rules, not redesign).

Terminology — if a later slide uses a term not defined by then, that is our bug

Graph theory II — knowledge level

Why graphs at all? The translator between messy human knowledge and machine precision

  • Agents fail on mush. Human knowledge arrives as prose, slides and memories — ambiguous, redundant, sometimes contradictory. Acting on it directly is exactly where AI "hallucination" lives.
  • A graph is the cheapest universal formalization: things become nodes, claimed relationships become edges you can enumerate, count and test. Circuits already are graphs; we made the knowledge and the audit trail graphs too.
  • One mathematics, three jobs — understand, derive, record — which is the monograph's own thesis, applied to the laboratory that studies it.

Experimental procedure: seven steps, three audits

  • 1 Question set by the PI — one sentence suffices.
  • 2 Pre-flight: archive retrieval + canonical-priors declaration.
  • 3 Execution on real MATLAB/Simulink via MCP.
  • 4 Acceptance criteria frozen before execution.
  • 5 Review: referee reproduction + challenger probes.
  • 6 Deposition: tests, clauses, knowledge, archive entry.
  • 7 Human consulted only for arbitration or agenda change.

Case study — a five-level current-source inverter nobody had built before

The engineering question: the lab's graph-theory research programme derives a new five-level current-source topology on paper via a graph-based translation. Can the AI take it from paper to a running, closed-loop, grid-quality machine — and how do we know each step is right?

paper topology
from the lab's graph-theory programme→ operating-state analysis
343 switch combos → 307 give 5 levels
→ 19 distinct output vectors→ current-sharing law
(see bullet 2)→ closed-loop control
on switched physics→ auditor recomputes
all 12 checks ✓

Closed-loop start-up from zero stored energy: the two independent DC-link currents are driven to the five-level operating point; rail balance held within ~0.7 A. Controller type: predictive switching (each cycle, simulate one step ahead for every candidate state and pick the best — no modulator needed).

  • Where do the passives come from? Directly from the translation: the VSC's phase inductors become the DC-link inductors, its DC-link capacitors become the AC-side capacitors. No component was "invented".
  • The surprise: the obvious assumption — DC current splits equally between parallel rails — is wrong for this topology. Analyzing which components are actually connected together in each switching state gives the correct sharing rule; prediction then matched simulation to 3×10⁻⁴ A.
  • All state counts (343 / 307 / 19) were exhaustively enumerated, not sampled.

Beyond running experiments: five tools that run the lab itself

The lab's operating loop. Solid arrows = each research cycle; dashed = new rules flow back into the next topic pick.

  • ① Topic picker: maintains 360 open questions (every converter variant × every translation × 12 question types) and ranks what is untouched, feasible and informative — by fixed rules, no hidden scoring.
  • ② Rule learner: when the same kind of mistake happens twice, it drafts a new lab rule from the recorded lessons; the professor approves or rejects with one word.
  • ④ Fair grader: checks results against quoted standards (e.g. IEEE 1547 THD ≤ 5%) and refuses apples-vs-oranges comparisons (different test bench → comparison blocked).
  • ⑤ Audit trail: next slides.

Each tool exists because the professor asked for it after a concrete incident — none is speculative architecture.

The meta-loop — errors become laws

Governance: honesty clauses and the human boundary

Standing rules

  • No quote, no fact; no measurement, no conclusion; numbers are never invented.
  • Refute by default: claims count only after surviving independent re-derivation.
  • Errors remain on the record with their errata — including the AI's own.
  • Full traceability: conclusion → code & data → knowledge entry → source page.
  • The knowledge base itself is falsifiable (correct-or-demote, with trace).

What remains human — and how little it now is

  • Standards supply is the irreducible core: which option, how strict, what counts as fulfilled. All 25 recorded interventions; the recent ones are one-line rulings (“1 approve; 2 partial…”, “e1:c; e2:a”, “批”).
  • Twice the PI chose a stricter option than both AI reviewers recommended — exactly the value the human-boundary theory predicts cannot be delegated.
  • Every decision request reaches the PI in a ratified format: full context, per-option downstream impact, plain language, stated recommendation.
  • Stated limitations: cold-start standards are human-set; the probe bank is field-calibrated; canonical coverage is finite.
